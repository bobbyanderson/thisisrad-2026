Disrupting the disruptor — redesigning how people send payments in a challenger banking app, from a multi-step process to something as natural as posting a social media update.

01 — Introduction

Disruption has to mean something.

When looking to disrupt an industry, a new product shouldn't have to rely on fancy branding or marketing to get people's attention — they can help, but that's not disruption. Real change comes from making users' lives genuinely easier. It's why products like Monzo or Uber earned the traction they did.

When working on B, Clydesdale Bank's challenger banking app, we felt there wasn't enough outside-the-box thinking — and too much replicating what already existed in legacy banking. So we took it upon ourselves to give B the RAD treatment, designing a feature that could let you pay someone in the time it takes to post a status update.

02 — The Problem

A challenger app with the same flows as the banks it was challenging.

B was launched as Clydesdale Bank's answer to the growing fintech disruption — a mobile-first banking app that looked different. But for us, it wasn't as disruptive as it could be. Many of its core user flows were identical to what you'd find in legacy banking applications.

Our research showed that people mainly use their mobile banking app for two things: to check their balance and to pay people. So why was one of those things orders of magnitude slower than the other? And why, in a new product explicitly designed to challenge the status quo, were we copying the same laborious journeys seen in traditional apps?

"We needed a new approach to paying contacts — one that feels as accessible and natural as sending a tweet."

03 — Our Role

Self-initiated, end to end.

As this was a self-initiated project, the concept was researched, visualised, prototyped and presented entirely by the RAD team. No brief, no client sign-off at each stage — just a genuine belief that this was a better way and the initiative to show it.

This case study is an example of seeing the bigger picture, making the most of available technology and delivering what users actually want. We pride ourselves on taking a different approach and coming up with ideas that add real value — not just executional ones.

04 — Design

On-brand, but genuinely new.

One thing we were deliberate about: staying within B's existing styleguide. We didn't want to introduce a visual language that felt alien to the product. The point was to show what could be possible within the existing system — not to reimagine the brand. This made the concept far more actionable for the team.

On the app's homepage, we implemented a row of avatars pulled from the user's contact list. Tapping a contact immediately triggers a quick payment flow to that individual — no navigation, no searching, no multi-step form.

We used a design pattern common in social media applications like Instagram: a horizontally scrollable row of contacts, ordered by most recently paid or most recently added. Familiar interaction, entirely new context.

05 — Lessons Learned

Ideas have value even when they don't ship.

The Quick Pay feature wasn't implemented — the demands on the development team and the existing product roadmap made it impractical at the time. But that's not the whole story.

Pitching ideas like this to senior stakeholders gave us invaluable insight into how decisions get made inside large organisations — what lands, what doesn't, and why. The feedback was strongly positive, and as a result we became a trusted voice when it came to innovating on what had already been built.

We've always found value in presenting ideas that show what's possible — approaching seemingly standard interactions, putting a new twist on them with a user-centred lens, and having the conviction to make the case.

06 — Future Scope

Good ideas are worth building on.

When presenting Quick Pay to stakeholders, we were explicit that what we'd designed was an MVP — a strong starting point with clear room to grow. One obvious iteration was account selection: the initial concept assumed payments came from the user's default account, but giving users control over the source account within the payment modal would be a meaningful improvement with relatively low engineering overhead.

Iterated designs showing account selection within the Quick Pay modal
07 — Conclusion

Small changes, big impact.

As with most projects, there's always room for improvement — and it's unlikely anyone gets it perfectly right on the first attempt. What this work demonstrated is that meaningful disruption doesn't require rebuilding a product from the ground up. It requires looking honestly at where users are frustrated, applying creative thinking to that specific friction, and having the courage to present something genuinely different.

At the time, no other fintech application had implemented a payment flow like this. That's the kind of opportunity that exists in almost every product — it just takes the willingness to look for it.
