Overview

Financial services is a sector where design decisions have real consequences.

The stakes are high — people's money, financial anxiety, credit records, and trust in institutions. It's also a sector where design is frequently treated as a decorative layer on top of business logic, rather than a strategic function that shapes outcomes.

These two projects sit at different ends of the spectrum: one a rigorous client engagement with a major bank, focused on eliminating friction from a high-stakes form journey; the other a self-initiated concept challenging what a modern payment experience could look like. Both were rooted in the same belief — that understanding users deeply produces better products and better business results.

Financial services has been a consistent thread throughout my career — across NatWest, RBS, Clydesdale Bank, Morgan Stanley, Deutsche Bank and M&G Prudential. The sector demands rigour, empathy and the ability to work within complex organisational structures without losing sight of the person on the other side of the screen.

Project 01 — NatWest & RBS

Creating credit card application forms with empathy.

RBS were moving away from legacy systems and needed a faster, more modern way to apply for a credit card online. Multiple teams across the bank were working on similar journeys — credit cards, current accounts, loans — with a shared design system intended to keep things consistent and reduce duplicated engineering effort.

The stakeholders had analytics data showing where drop-off was occurring. They had assumptions about why. Those assumptions were wrong.

"Analytical data only tells so much of the story — and it's a very dehumanised story. It's easy to skew numbers to suit an agenda. Research puts people back in the picture."

Before any design work began, we ran research sessions — inviting users to test the existing journeys on mobile, tablet and desktop. What we found didn't match the stakeholder assumptions at all. The problems were in the language, the flow structure, the anxiety around credit checks, and the unnecessary steps forced on users who didn't need them.

One finding in particular stood out: users knew what a credit check was, but had no idea at what point in the journey it would happen or what the impact on their record would be. That uncertainty was causing drop-off — not the form fields themselves.

The Approach

Change how the organisation thinks, then change the product.

My role was Lead UX Designer on the credit card application journey, working alongside a UX writer and service designer, collaborating with journey managers and wider stakeholders. Part of the work was design. Part of it was getting a numbers-first organisation to genuinely consider the human context behind those numbers.

We worked in an agile environment — scoping and sizing based on research findings, prioritising the pages and sections that would have the most impact. The bank's existing design system meant we could iterate rapidly without rebuilding component foundations from scratch.

Responsive design tested across mobile, tablet and desktop — critical for an application journey used on all three
Design Decisions

Modular journeys and progressive disclosure.

The modular journey

The old flow forced users through setup steps for features they might not want — direct debit setup, balance transfers, adding a cardholder. This created unnecessary friction and length for the majority of applicants. We proposed an opt-in modular structure: surface these options clearly, but only expand them for users who choose to engage. Tested well. Reduced perceived length significantly.

The opt-in modular journey — users choose which features to set up during the application rather than being forced through every step

The middle name problem

A small detail that reveals a lot. During testing, we repeatedly observed users who navigated by keyboard tabbing from first name directly into the middle name field — and entering their surname there by mistake. Some caught it. Many didn't until the last name field felt wrong.

The fix was simple: hide the middle name field behind a checkbox. Users who have a middle name opt in, the field expands, they fill it in. Users who don't have one never see an empty field waiting to trip them up. A tiny interaction change with meaningful impact — and because it was a shared component, the fix fed through to the current account and loan journeys too.

Progressive disclosure for the middle name field — opt-in rather than always-present, eliminating a consistent point of user error
Outcome

Better for users. Better for the business.

Design thinking embedded into a function that was used to working on business metrics alone. Assumptions challenged. Research-backed decisions made. And results that made the case for doing it again.

Project 02 — Clydesdale Bank · Self-initiated

A challenger bank that wasn't challenging enough.

B was Clydesdale Bank's attempt at a fintech-era mobile banking app — fresh brand, modern look, younger target audience. But when we looked at the actual product, the core user journeys were identical to the legacy banking apps it was supposedly replacing.

Research consistently shows mobile banking users primarily do two things: check their balance and pay people. Checking a balance takes seconds. Making a payment required navigating multiple screens, searching for contacts, confirming details — a process far more complex than it needed to be. For a product positioning itself as disruptive, that was a significant missed opportunity.

The Concept

Payments as fast as posting a status update.

We designed a Quick Pay feature — entirely self-initiated, researched, visualised, prototyped and presented by the team. The concept: bring a row of contact avatars directly to the app's home screen, ordered by most recently paid. Tap a contact. Pay them. Done.

The interaction pattern was borrowed from social apps like Instagram — a familiar swipeable row of people. Placed it in a banking context, and suddenly a 5-step process became a single tap.

Crucially, we stayed within B's existing styleguide throughout. This wasn't a rebrand — it was a demonstration of what the existing product could become, presented in terms the development and product teams could actually act on.

Quick Pay — contact avatars on the home screen, ordered by most recently paid. One tap opens the payment modal.
Iterated designs — account selection added to the modal so users control where the payment comes from
Outcome

Ideas have value even when they don't ship immediately.

The feature wasn't implemented — competing development priorities at the time made it impractical. But the pitch to senior stakeholders was well received, and RAD became a trusted voice for product innovation within the organisation. No other fintech application had implemented a payments flow like this at the time.

First of its kind in fintech at the time Strong senior stakeholder reception Became ongoing innovation partner
Takeaways

What financial services taught me about design.

Financial products sit at the intersection of high user anxiety and high organisational caution. The combination tends to produce conservative, friction-heavy experiences — because nobody gets fired for adding a confirmation step. Getting better outcomes requires earning the trust to challenge that thinking.

Both of these projects required as much work on stakeholder alignment and organisational mindset as they did on the actual design. That's not a complaint — it's the job. And when it works, the results show up in the numbers.
