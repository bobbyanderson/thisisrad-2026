Scout IQ

A data-driven football scouting platform built solo — to explore AI-assisted development, and to answer a question the existing tools weren't asking: not how a player performs, but how they could.

The problem

Sports analytics has a clarity problem.

The data available to football clubs has exploded over the last decade. Touches, progressive carries, xG, pressures, PPDA — the volume of information is extraordinary. But most tools that surface this data are built for analysts with the time and context to interpret it, not for scouts who need to make fast, confident decisions across leagues they may not know deeply.

Existing platforms showed plenty of evidence of what a player had done. Very few had anything useful to say about what a player could do — and none of the accessible tools translated that into the language scouts actually use: value, potential, and risk.

The question every scout is really asking isn't "how good is this player?" It's "how good could this player be — and what would they cost in 18 months?"

That gap was the brief. I didn't set out to build another stats aggregator. I set out to build a tool that made data meaningful for the person making the decision, not the analyst sitting behind them.

The insight

Reframe the question — from performance to potential.

The fundamental shift Scout IQ makes is a product-level one: performance data tells you what has happened. What scouts need is a forward signal — trajectory, ceiling, and the monetary implication of both.

A 22-year-old striker averaging 0.4 goals per 90 in the Championship and trending upward is a very different proposition to a 29-year-old with the same numbers in the same league. The raw stat is identical. The decision should be completely different. This is the gap most tools leave entirely to the scout's own judgement, which means the quality of the insight varies entirely with the experience of the person holding it.

Scout IQ's goal was to encode some of that expert judgement into the product itself — surfacing the right signal automatically, so the tool does more of the interpretive work and the scout arrives at the decision faster and with more confidence.

The scoring system

Five scores. Each answering a specific question.

The proprietary scoring system was the core design challenge. It wasn't just a data science problem — it was a communication design problem. How do you take three years of player data across 47 competitions and make it instantly readable to someone who has thirty seconds to decide if a player is worth a deeper look?

The answer was five distinct scores, each mapped to a real question a scout might ask, rather than a general-purpose number that requires interpretation.

ScoutIQ Index

What is this player's overall quality?

A 0–99 composite score, weighted by position. The headline number — calibrated to feel intuitive: 90+ is elite, 70+ is reliable top-flight, 50+ is a player worth watching.

Growth Score

What is this player worth in 12–24 months?

The most important score. It combines age profile, output trajectory, and injury risk to produce a value signal — not just a performance one. A player scoring 8+ here is an appreciating asset. This is the number a club's recruitment team actually cares about.

Attack Score

How productive are they in the final third?

Goals, assists and key passes per 90, adjusted for league tier and position. Gives context on attacking output without penalising defenders or defensive midfielders for not scoring.

Defence Score

How much do they contribute defensively?

Tackles, interceptions and blocks per 90. Provides the counterbalance to attack output — particularly useful for evaluating full-backs, box-to-box midfielders, and forwards who press.

Availability

Can you rely on them?

Injury history across all tracked seasons, expressed as an availability percentage with a risk rating. Feeds directly into both the ScoutIQ Index and Growth Score — a brilliant player who misses 30% of games is not the same proposition as a consistent one.

Why five, not one?

A single composite score is a black box. Five scores let the scout interrogate the verdict — understanding whether a high index reflects pure attack, defensive solidity, or availability. The system is transparent by design.

The UX challenge

Making 37,000 players legible — without hiding the depth.

The information architecture challenge with Scout IQ was a familiar tension in data product design: how do you give users access to genuine depth without overwhelming them at the surface? With 37,700 players across 47 competitions and 3 seasons of data, the naive approach — show everything — produces something unusable. But hiding too much defeats the purpose of the tool.

The solution was a layered disclosure model. The index page surfaces the ScoutIQ score and a handful of key indicators — enough to make a fast judgment on whether a player merits attention. Clicking through reveals the full breakdown: position-specific sub-scores, percentile ranks against peers in the same league tier, trajectory charts across seasons, and a full injury log.

Surface level
Quick signal
Search, filter by position, league, age or nationality. Results sorted by ScoutIQ Index. A scout can scan 50 players in two minutes and identify three worth a deeper look.
Player detail
Full breakdown
All five scores, sub-scores by position, percentile ranks within tier and age group, season-on-season trajectory charts, and complete injury history.
Compare
Side-by-side decisions
Direct comparison tables and radar chart overlays for up to two players — because scouting decisions are almost never about one player in isolation, they're about choosing between two.

The comparison feature was a deliberate product decision, not an afterthought. Scouts rarely need to find "the best player" in the abstract — they need to make a choice between a shortlist. Building the comparison directly into the core flow, rather than as a secondary report, reflects how the decision actually gets made.

Favourites and notes let scouts organise players into custom groups across scouting windows — treating the tool as a working environment, not just a lookup table.

What the build taught me

Designer as product owner — and what that changes.

Scout IQ was built using Claude as a development collaborator — what's come to be called vibe coding. I had a clear product vision and strong design instincts, but limited prior experience with the full stack it required. Working with an AI to write and iterate on the implementation meant I could move from idea to live product without a development team, but it also put every product decision squarely on me.

There's a meaningful difference between designing a product for someone else to build and owning the whole thing. When you're responsible for both, every scope decision has a cost you feel directly. Features I might have added as a designer without hesitation became questions I had to weigh properly: does this serve the user, or does it serve my idea of what the tool should be?

Building it solo made me a more considered product designer. Every decision had to earn its place — there was no team to socialise it past and no engineer to push back on the complexity.

The process also introduced me to a stack I hadn't worked with in depth before — Supabase for the database and auth layer, Vercel for deployment, Next.js for the front-end. Getting comfortable with these tools changed how I talk to engineering teams. I now understand the decisions they're making, not just the outcomes. That's worth more than any individual feature I shipped.

What I'd do differently: proper user research before the scoring model was finalised. I designed the five scores from a logical framework — what questions matter to scouts — but I built it without talking to working scouts. The logic holds, but validation from real users would sharpen it significantly. That's the next phase.

Try it

Scout IQ is live and free to explore.

The platform is live at footballscout.vercel.app. Free accounts get five player searches to explore the tool — enough to get a feel for the scoring system and the comparison features. No credit card required.

This is a product I continue to iterate on. The data updates, the scoring model is under ongoing review, and a proper user research phase is on the roadmap. It started as a learning project and became something I actually use.
